Clauses

English grammar can be tricky, so it's best to start from the basics. This lesson covers defining clauses, how to identify them, how to create them, how to use them, and why they are important to learn.

A clause is: a group of words that has both a subject and a verb

Remember!

A subject is: The noun that is doing or being in a sentence. In other words, who/what the sentence is about

A noun is: a person, place, thing, or idea

A verb is: an action, a word that indicates a state of being

There are two types of clauses: independent and dependent

Independent clauses: Has a subject, a verb, and expresses a complete thought

Dependent clauses: Has a subject, a verb, but does not express a complete thought

A complete thought: some happened, something was said

Dependent clause must be joined with an independent clause in order to be a proper sentence. Independent clauses may also join with another independent clause if punctuated correctly.

Joined Independent Clauses

The beach is a lot of fun, but the mountains are even better.

I went to the mall, but forgot to get socks.

We all looked very tired, for we had stayed up all night cramming for the final.

I was very happy; I had pizza and ice cream.

Joined Independent and Dependent Clauses

The beach is a lot of fun, but the mountains are even better.

I went to the mall, but forgot to get socks.

We all looked very tired, for we had stayed up all night cramming for the final.

I was very happy; I had pizza and ice cream.
